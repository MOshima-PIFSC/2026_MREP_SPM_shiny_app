## Welcome to the WP-MREP Stock Assessment Simulation Tool

The Magnuson-Steven Fishery Conservation and Management Act requires decisions to manage federal stocks are based on the best scientific information available (BSIA). The scientific information used for these decisions are called stock assessments. The process of generating stock assessments starts with the fishers who generate fishery statistics while they are conducting their fishing activities. These statistics are collected by the local fisheries agencies in the Pacific Islands region, through a grant provided by the federal government, and shared with the Pacific Islands Fisheries Science Center through a data sharing agreement.

This Stock Assessment Simulation Tool will guide the MREP participants through the fishery science generation process. The goal of this simulation activity is to introduce the participants to the following concepts:
- Data collection
- Basic stock assessment
- Trends and patterns
- Stock status

The participants will be divided into five groups. Each group assigned a target species with its specific life history characteristics:
- Yellowfin tuna
- Mahi-mahi
- Opakapaka
- Peacock grouper
- Yellowfin goatfish

The group will simulate three fisheries scenario:
- Healthy
- Overfished
- Recovering

Members of each group will be assigned the following roles in this exercise:
- **Fisher** - will fish for ball bearings (representing the fish) using a rod with line and magnet
- **Data collector** - will count the number of ball bearings "caught" in the time allowed
- **Observer** - will observe the fisher fishing behavior and report observations
- **Assessment analyst** - run the stock assessment and report on trends

Each group will report on their respective fisheries and the plenary will have a group discussion.

### Definitions to get you started  

🐟 **Stock** - a species or a group of species that is managed under a fishery management plan.

📈 **r (Population Growth Rate)** - the rate at which the size of a population can increase under ideal conditions (aka, no predators, unlimited food supply, unlimited habitat, etc.)

🌊 **K (Carrying Capacity)** - the maximum number of fish a habitat can support (based on how much food, space, etc. is available).

**q (Catchability)** - how "catchable" a stock is.

🎣 **CPUE (Catch per unit Effort)** - the amount of fish caught relative to the amount of effort (aka hours spent fishing or number of hooks used). CPUE is used to track changes in stock size. If you are catching more fish in the same amount of effort over time, this can mean the stock is increasing. Conversely, if you are catching less fish than you used to in the same amount of effort, then this can mean the stock is going down.

🎯 **MSY (Maximum Sustainable Yield)** - the most catch a fish population can support year after year without affecting future catches.

🐠 **Bmsy (Biomass at Maximum Sustainable Yield)** - the total amount of fish needed to keep catching at MSY.

🪝 **Fmsy (Fishing Mortality at Maximum Sustainable Yield)** - the amount of fishing that gives you MSY. Fishing more than this will hurt future catches.

🚨 **Overfished** - A stock that is too low (not enough fish).

⚠️ **Overfishing** - The rate of fishing is too high to maintain a healthy stock (too much fishing).

> A stock can be *overfished* but *overfishing* isn't occurring, or *overfishing* can be occurring but the stock is not yet considered *overfished*. 

### Relevant Information on Species  

**Yellowfin Tuna (Ahi)**

Yellowfin Tuna, aka Ahi, are a highly mobile species. They can travel thousands of miles across the Pacific ocean to reach food and spawning grounds. Yellowfin Tuna grow fast and are relatively short-lived, they live about 6 to 7 years. Their populations increase at a moderate rate and because they are widely distributed across the Pacific, their populations are relatively resilient to fishing pressure. However, they do spawn in large aggregations which can make them vulnerable to fishing. Yellowfin tuna are considered epipelagic, meaning they are found in the open ocean up to about 100 m down.

**Mahi Mahi**  

Mahi Mahi are an extremely fast growing and maturing fish and can live about 4 to 5 years. Because of this "live fast, die young" strategy, their populations are highly resilient to fishing pressure and they can recover quickly from fishing pressure due to their fast growth, early maturation, and frequent spawning (multiple times a year). Mahi Mahi are typically found near the surface in tropical and subtropical waters of the open ocean.

**Opakapaka**  

Opakapaka are a relatively slow growing, long-lived species. They can live over 40 years and reach maturity around 2 years of age. Because of their slower development and longer life spans, their populations are more vulnerable to heavy fishing pressure and if the stock size is reduced, recovery can take much longer. **Peacock Grouper** 

Peacock Grouper, aka Roi, are an invasive grouper found on many reefs around the Hawaiian Islands. Like many grouper species, Peacock Grouper are hermaphroditic, meaning at some point in their life they switch from a female to male. Because they carry a bacteria known as ciguatera, they cannot be fished and sold commercially. This lack of fishing pressure along with their status as a top predator on reefs, has allowed their populations to dominate many local reefs. Community efforts and fishing competitions can be one way to remove them from the reefs. 

**Yellowfin Goatfish (Weke 'Ula)**

Yellowfin Goatfish, aka Weke 'Ula, are a short-lived fast maturing, small, species. They only live up to about 5 years, and are highly resilient to fishing pressure. They can spawn throughout the year which helps to maintain their populations against fishing pressure. They are found in shallow sandy bottoms near reefs.
